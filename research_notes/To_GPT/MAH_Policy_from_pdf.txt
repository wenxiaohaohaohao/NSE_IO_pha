Institutional Reform and Pharmaceutical Innovation
       An Analysis Based on the MAH System
              Danni Yangchen, Wenxiao Dong, Xinhao Wang




                                       Abstract



    We develop a dynamic industry model to study how China's Marketing Authoriza-
 tion Holder (MAH) reform changes rm boundaries, organizational transitions, and
 pharmaceutical innovation. We model two medicine categories, generic drugs and
 original drugs. Integrated rms can produce both medicines and conduct original-
 drug research. Manufacturing-specialized rms can produce both medicines but do
 not originate new original-drug ideas. Research-specialized rms generate original-drug
 ideas only. Before MAH, research-specialized rms cannot independently commercialize
 original-drug ideas through external production while retaining marketing authoriza-
 tion; after MAH, they can commercialize through qualied external manufacturers. We
 build the model on a Hopenhayn-style stationary industry shell with endogenous entry
 and exit, convex research eort for original-drug discovery, and a qualied contract-
 manufacturing market for MAH-enabled commercialization. We dene innovation using
 both the number of new original-drug products commercialized and the share of original
 drugs in all commercialized products. The equilibrium delivers predictions for incum-
 bent transition matrices, entry composition, producer-side outcomes, and innovation
 structure in China's pharmaceutical industry.

             I. INTRODUCTION AND LITERATURE STRATEGY
We study whether MAH relaxes the institutional bundle between original-drug research and


production capacity, thereby changing organizational boundaries and increasing original-


drug commercialization in China's pharmaceutical industry. We focus on the pharmaceu-


tical industry rather than the whole economy.    Accordingly, the surplus object below is a


pharmaceutical-industry surplus object rather than an economy-wide welfare object.


    We organize the model around ve literature blocks.       First, we use the stationary


heterogeneous-rm shell in Hopenhayn (1992).      That shell also connects to the selection


logic in Jovanovic (1982), the dynamic-control language in Ericson and Pakes (1995), and


the composition perspective in Luttmer (2007). Second, we use Klette and Kortum (2004)


and Akcigit and Kerr (2018) to motivate convex original-drug research eort and compo-


sition eects on industry outcomes. Third, we use Arora et al. (2001), Arora and Merges


(2004), Gans and Stern (2000), Gans and Stern (2003), and Gans et al. (2008) to motivate


why innovative rms need not be vertically integrated in order to create value.    Fourth,


we use pharmaceutical-policy references such as Lakdawalla (2018), Chandra et al. (2026),


and Jia et al. (2023) to motivate why regulatory design can change commercialization and


innovation without acting only through prices.   Fifth, we borrow only the distinction be-


tween invention and implementation from Fons-Rosen et al. (2026); we do not borrow their


acquisition, search, or bargaining layers.


    This modeling strategy matches our empirical question.      MAH is not a merger pol-


icy, and our data do not identify bargaining primitives.   We therefore need an upstream


original-drug idea-generation margin, a downstream commercialization margin, a qualied


contract-manufacturing market, and organizational switching across integrated, research-


specialized, and manufacturing-specialized rms. We do not need an acquisition block or a


full incomplete-contract model.

A. What we borrow and what we change
We borrow the incumbent value-function logic, free-entry condition, and stationary distri-


bution from Hopenhayn (1992), but we replace a single rm type with three organizational


states because our question is about rm boundaries. We borrow convex research eort from


Klette and Kortum (2004), but we apply it only to original-drug idea generation rather than


to generic expansion. We borrow the composition logic from Akcigit and Kerr (2018), but


we apply it to sectoral reorganization rather than long-run growth accounting. We borrow


commercialization and specialization logic from Arora and Merges (2004) and Gans and


Stern (2003), but we translate them into an MAH-specic outsourcing environment. Finally,


we borrow the invention-versus-implementation distinction from Fons-Rosen et al. (2026),


but we replace startup acquisition with MAH-enabled commercialization through qualied


external manufacturing.


    These changes are substantive rather than cosmetic. They make the model t China's


pharmaceutical industry and the MAH institution while preserving the literature's core mech-


anisms.

                         IV. TIMING WITHIN A PERIOD
We adapt the timing from dynamic industry models such as Ericson and Pakes (1995), while


keeping the stationary entry-and-exit shell of Hopenhayn (1992). We choose current-period


organization before current-period operating choices because MAH changes the same-period


feasibility of outsourcing versus internal commercialization.


    Within each period, events occur in the following order.



 1. A rm enters the period with current organizational type s ∈ {A, B, C} and current


    capability z .



 2. The rm chooses its current-period organizational form j ∈ J (s), where the feasible set


    allows it to remain in its current form, switch to either of the other two forms, or exit.


    If j ̸= s, the rm pays switching cost κsj (M ) immediately.



 3. Conditional on the chosen current-period organizational form j , the rm chooses current


    operating controls and earns current operating payo Πj .


                                                                                     ′
 4. If the rm does not exit, next period's capability state is drawn from Qj ( dz       | z, M ).

 5. Potential entrants draw initial capability from G and choose whether to enter as type


    A, B , or C .

    This timing is the cleanest way to keep the MAH outsourcing margin and the


organizational-boundary margin inside the same-period problem.       It also lets us write the


value functions in the standard current-ow-plus-continuation form.

D. Identication logic
We discipline   β using a standard annual discount factor.    We pin down the innovation-


curvature parameters using the dispersion of original-drug research eort and original-drug

state-dynamics parameters and pre-MAH switching costs to match the pre-MAH transition


matrix and type shares. We choose the ow-payo parameters to match pre-MAH generic


and original-drug outcomes, producer-side outcomes, and entry composition.        We choose


the MAH shifts in commercialization friction and the commercialization-success function


λB to match post-MAH changes in the transition matrix, the number of new original-drug
products, the share of original drugs, commercialization proxies, and entry composition.


    The key point is that the transition matrix and the original-drug share are not auxiliary


outputs. They are two of the main identifying objects of the model.



E. Practical estimation
A practical strategy is simulated method of moments or indirect inference. We discretize the


capability state using a standard Markov approximation, solve the stationary equilibrium


under   M = 0 and M = 1, simulate rm histories, compute model-implied type shares,
transition matrices, entrant composition, new original-drug counts, and original-drug shares,


and choose the parameter vector to minimize the weighted distance between model and data.

Assumption 1 (Regularity). Z is compact. The transition kernels Qj (· | z, M ) are weakly
continuous in z . Operating payo functions are bounded and continuous in their state and

price arguments.

Assumption 2 (Convexity). η > 1. The cost function c(m, z; w) is convex in m and
continuous.

increasing in [HB ]+ , optimized type-B current payo rises weakly. The Bellman equation


for   VB then implies that the value of operating as a research-specialized rm also rises
weakly.

 2. We let   integrated rms and manufacturing-specialized rms produce both
