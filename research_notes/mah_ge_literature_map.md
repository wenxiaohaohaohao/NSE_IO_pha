# Literature Map for the MAH GE Paper

This note organizes the literature into five blocks. For each block, the key
question is not only "what should be cited" but also "what role the citation
plays in the paper."

## 1. Firm dynamics and selection: the shell for entry, exit, and state distributions

### Core references you should definitely cite

1. Hugo A. Hopenhayn (1992), "Entry, Exit, and Firm Dynamics in Long Run Equilibrium," *Econometrica* 60(5): 1127-1150.  
   Link: https://www.econometricsociety.org/publications/econometrica/1992/09/01/entry-exit-and-firm-dynamics-long-run-equilibrium  
   Use it for:
   - stationary equilibrium with entry and exit
   - firm selection logic
   - distributional view of industry structure
   Why it matters here:
   - your first theory draft is best framed as a Hopenhayn-style stationary equilibrium with organizational states

2. Boyan Jovanovic (1982), "Selection and the Evolution of Industry," *Econometrica* 50(3): 649-670.  
   Link: https://www.econometricsociety.org/publications/econometrica/1982/05/01/selection-and-evolution-industry  
   Use it for:
   - learning, survival, and selection
   - why small or uncertain firms are more likely to exit or transition
   Why it matters here:
   - gives a natural interpretation for why some entrants remain specialized while others integrate or exit

3. Richard Ericson and Ariel Pakes (1995), "Markov-Perfect Industry Dynamics: A Framework for Empirical Work," *Review of Economic Studies* 62(1): 53-82.  
   Link: https://academic.oup.com/restud/article-abstract/62/1/53/1568000  
   Use it for:
   - dynamic industry models with firm-specific uncertainty
   - investment and state transitions in equilibrium
   Why it matters here:
   - supports a richer state-transition language than a pure cost-reduction story

4. Erzo G. J. Luttmer (2007), "Selection, Growth, and the Size Distribution of Firms," *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 122(3): 1103-1144.  
   Link: https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/122/3/1103/1879504  
   Use it for:
   - selection and growth in an equilibrium distribution
   - how entry and imitation affect aggregate performance
   Why it matters here:
   - useful when you discuss how MAH may change the long-run composition of firm types

### How this block should appear in your paper

- Introduction: "This paper contributes to the literature on firm dynamics by embedding organizational form transitions into a stationary industry equilibrium."
- Model section: cite Hopenhayn most heavily.
- Mechanism section: cite Ericson-Pakes if you want a more general dynamic-industry framing.

## 2. Innovation in dynamic equilibrium: where the GE language comes from

### Core references

1. Tor Jakob Klette and Samuel Kortum (2004), "Innovating Firms and Aggregate Innovation," *Journal of Political Economy* 112(5): 986-1018.  
   Link: https://www.minneapolisfed.org/research/staff-reports/innovating-firms-and-aggregate-innovation  
   Use it for:
   - firm-level innovation in an aggregate equilibrium
   - connecting firm heterogeneity and aggregate innovation
   Why it matters here:
   - this is one of the cleanest ways to justify moving from PE to industry GE

2. Ufuk Akcigit and William R. Kerr (2018), "Growth through Heterogeneous Innovations," *Journal of Political Economy* 126(4): 1374-1443.  
   Link: https://dash.harvard.edu/entities/publication/af108373-c1f5-4a9f-851a-15082aa592d5  
   Use it for:
   - internal vs external innovation
   - innovation heterogeneity across incumbents and entrants
   - growth consequences of firm composition
   Why it matters here:
   - extremely useful for arguing that organizational structure changes the type of innovation, not just the amount

3. Kenneth J. Arrow (1962), "Economic Welfare and the Allocation of Resources for Invention," in *The Rate and Direction of Inventive Activity*.  
   Link: https://www.nber.org/books-and-chapters/rate-and-direction-inventive-activity-economic-and-social-factors/economic-welfare-and-allocation-resources-invention  
   Use it for:
   - replacement effect
   - welfare tradeoffs in innovation policy
   Why it matters here:
   - essential when you explain why incumbent profit and total innovation may move differently

4. Philippe Aghion, Nick Bloom, Richard Blundell, Rachel Griffith, and Peter Howitt (2005), "Competition and Innovation: An Inverted-U Relationship," *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 120(2): 701-728.  
   Link: https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/120/2/701/1933966  
   Use it for:
   - innovation incentives under competition
   - non-monotonicity in market structure and innovation
   Why it matters here:
   - helpful for cautioning that stronger specialization or competition need not always raise innovation one-for-one

### How this block should appear in your paper

- Use Klette-Kortum and Akcigit-Kerr in the model motivation.
- Use Arrow and Aghion et al. in the welfare and comparative statics discussion.

## 3. Firm boundaries, market for ideas, and technology commercialization

This is the block your current PPT is missing most clearly.

### Core references

1. Ashish Arora, Andrea Fosfuri, and Alfonso Gambardella (2001), *Markets for Technology: The Economics of Innovation and Corporate Strategy*. MIT Press.  
   Link: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262011907/  
   Use it for:
   - market for technology
   - licensing, specialization, and the division of innovative labor
   Why it matters here:
   - this is the broad conceptual anchor for why R&D firms do not need to be integrated producers

2. Ashish Arora and Robert P. Merges (2004), "Specialized Supply Firms, Property Rights and Firm Boundaries," *Industrial and Corporate Change* 13(3): 451-475.  
   Link: https://academic.oup.com/icc/article-abstract/13/3/451/703329  
   Use it for:
   - how property rights shape make-or-buy decisions
   - why stronger rights can support specialized suppliers
   Why it matters here:
   - directly relevant for arguing that MAH changes the efficient boundary of the firm

3. Joshua S. Gans and Scott Stern (2000), "Incumbency and R&D Incentives: Licensing the Gale of Creative Destruction," *Journal of Economics & Management Strategy* 9(4): 485-511.  
   Link: https://www.kellogg.northwestern.edu/academics-research/research/detail/2000/incumbency-and-rd-incentives-licensing-the-gale-of-creative/  
   Use it for:
   - entrant innovation versus incumbent innovation with licensing
   - bargaining and commercialization environment
   Why it matters here:
   - ideal for your B-firm commercialization and incumbent-profit discussion

4. Joshua S. Gans and Scott Stern (2003), "The Product Market and the Market for 'Ideas': Commercialization Strategies for Technology Entrepreneurs," *Research Policy* 32(2): 333-350.  
   Link: https://www.kellogg.northwestern.edu/faculty/research/detail/2003/the-product-market-and-the-market-for-ideas-commercialization/  
   Use it for:
   - why some innovators compete and others cooperate with incumbents
   - commercialization choice under different environments
   Why it matters here:
   - highly relevant to "biotech self-commercialize vs outsource vs integrate"

5. Joshua S. Gans, David H. Hsu, and Scott Stern (2008), "The Impact of Uncertain Intellectual Property Rights on the Market for Ideas: Evidence from Patent Grant Delays," *Management Science* 54(5): 982-997.  
   Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w13234  
   Use it for:
   - imperfect market for ideas
   - importance of clarified rights for licensing / cooperation
   Why it matters here:
   - MAH can be framed as clarifying commercialization and responsibility rights, not only as lowering cost

6. Josh Lerner and Robert P. Merges (1998), "The Control of Technology Alliances: An Empirical Analysis of the Biotechnology Industry," *Journal of Industrial Economics* 46(2): 125-156.  
   Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w6014  
   Use it for:
   - biotech-pharma cooperation
   - allocation of control rights in alliances
   Why it matters here:
   - this is one of the most directly relevant industry papers when you talk about biotech and manufacturing incumbents

### How this block should appear in your paper

- End of introduction: this is where you explain the paper's marginal contribution.
- Mechanism section: this block justifies why MAH should change organizational form rather than merely the level of R&D.
- Discussion section: use these citations to show your story fits a broader literature on commercialization under incomplete markets.

## 4. Pharmaceutical innovation, regulation, and policy

### Broad demand, patent, and policy references

1. Daron Acemoglu and Joshua Linn (2004), "Market Size in Innovation: Theory and Evidence from the Pharmaceutical Industry," *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 119(3): 1049-1090.  
   Link: https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/119/3/1049/1938820  
   Use it for:
   - market size and pharmaceutical innovation
   Why it matters here:
   - helps distinguish demand-side incentives from your organizational mechanism

2. Amy Finkelstein (2004), "Static and Dynamic Effects of Health Policy: Evidence from the Vaccine Industry," *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 119(2): 527-564.  
   Link: https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/119/2/527/1894514  
   Use it for:
   - policy-induced innovation
   - dynamic welfare effects
   Why it matters here:
   - a nice precedent for not equating static market effects with long-run innovation effects

3. Darius Lakdawalla (2018), "Economics of the Pharmaceutical Industry," *Journal of Economic Literature* 56(2): 397-449.  
   Link: https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:aea:jeclit:v:56:y:2018:i:2:p:397-449  
   Use it for:
   - broad survey of pricing, innovation, and pharmaceutical organization
   Why it matters here:
   - this is a useful general-citation backbone in the literature review

4. Bhaven Sampat and Heidi L. Williams (2019), "How Do Patents Affect Follow-On Innovation? Evidence from the Human Genome," *American Economic Review* 109(1): 203-236.  
   Link: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20151398  
   Use it for:
   - property rights and follow-on innovation
   Why it matters here:
   - useful if you decide to discuss original innovation vs follow-on imitation more carefully

5. Amitabh Chandra, Jennifer Kao, Kathleen L. Miller, and Ariel D. Stern (2022), "Regulatory Incentives for Innovation: The FDA's Breakthrough Therapy Designation," NBER Working Paper 30712.  
   Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w30712  
   Use it for:
   - targeted regulatory design and innovation
   - regulatory institutions as innovation policy
   Why it matters here:
   - good support for the claim that regulatory design can affect innovation without necessarily acting only through price incentives

6. Ruixue Jia, Xiao Ma, Jianan Yang, and Yiran Zhang (2023), "Improving Regulation for Innovation: Evidence from China's Pharmaceutical Industry," NBER Working Paper 31976.  
   Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w31976  
   Local copy: [Local Jia PDF](D:/working_paper/DANNI/Jia%20%E7%AD%89%20-%202023%20-%20Improving%20Regulation%20for%20Innovation%20Evidence%20from.pdf)  
   Use it for:
   - China's 2015 regulatory reform
   - approval-time mechanism
   - composition change through entry
   Why it matters here:
   - your paper should cite it prominently and then say: "we focus on MAH and organizational boundaries, not approval-speed alone"

### How this block should appear in your paper

- Use Acemoglu-Linn and Finkelstein early to position the policy-innovation literature.
- Use Jia et al. in both introduction and literature review.
- Make the distinction very explicit: "approval speed" is not the only regulatory margin; "ownership/commercialization organization" is another.

## 5. Pharma-specific commercialization, competition, and acquisition

These are not the core shell of your model, but they are excellent discussion references.

1. Colleen Cunningham, Florian Ederer, and Song Ma (2021), "Killer Acquisitions," *Journal of Political Economy* 129(3): 649-702.  
   Link: https://som.yale.edu/node/267623  
   Use it for:
   - pharma acquisitions and innovation suppression
   - commercialization by incumbents versus independent development
   Why it matters here:
   - useful when discussing whether cooperation with incumbents always benefits innovation

2. Christian Fons-Rosen, Pau Roldan-Blanco, and Tom Schmitz (2024), "The Effects of Startup Acquisitions on Innovation and Economic Growth," working paper version dated 2024-05-07.  
   Local copy: [Local FRS PDF](D:/working_paper/DANNI/frs_may2024.pdf)  
   Use it for:
   - `startup rate`
   - `incumbent innovation`
   - `implementation/commercialization rate`
   - GE decomposition
   Why it matters here:
   - you should borrow the decomposition logic, not the acquisition-specific institutional detail

3. Lee Branstetter, Chirantan Chatterjee, and Matthew J. Higgins (2022), "Generic Competition and the Incentives for Early-Stage Pharmaceutical Innovation," *Research Policy* 51(10): 104595.  
   Link: https://www.nber.org/papers/w20532  
   Use it for:
   - how downstream competitive conditions affect upstream innovation
   Why it matters here:
   - relevant if you later add more discussion of incumbent producer profits and innovation incentives

### How this block should appear in your paper

- Mostly in the literature review and discussion.
- Use it to show you are aware that commercialization and downstream structure can affect innovation incentives.
- Do not let this block crowd out the main organizational-boundary mechanism.

without getting lost in side literatures.
